Hey Oldy

In a preivious thread I remember reading about using the planer to clean-up your template cut.
Sounds weird, but trust me, it really works good. I've tried it and was very impressed. Do it all the time now.

Cut out the template with your hand saw about 1/8" to 1/4" outside your line.
Set-up the blank with foam off-cuts so it sits vertical in the stands
Set your planer on the lowest setting. On mine it is 1.
Run your planer along your saw cut from about 6" to 8" from the nose to the same in the tail.
Just balance the planer on the blades as you pass
As you plane along your saw cut, the planer will plane off the high spots and not cut the low spots.
After a few passes, you will get a nice even cut the full length off the rail.
As you get closer to your pencil line, set your planer to 0 and you can do some fine tuning and go closer to the nose and tail until you are right on your line.
I always try to finish off wth 1 complete pass from about 2" from the nose to the same in the tail.
You can then finish off the nose and tail with the sanding block.
One important thing to remember is to keep the planer 90 degrees to the bottom of the blank.
I am going to try out the jig guide that came with my planer.

Try it out, trust me, it works.
I will be starting a new 7'6" big guy tri for myself in the next couple of weeks and will be posting pics of my progress.
I will make sure I put in this step for you.

Cheers

MDS

ever had 'shaper's block', or is it just me?

happened again last night. everything was in order: my boy was in bed, kitchen cleaned up, i'd ironed my work clothes & made my lunch for the next day. poured myself a glass of red & went downstairs full of expectation to shape. i'd been thinking about it all day. but all of a sudden, a few passes with the surform into what should have been a productive couple of hours work, my resolve faltered. suddenly the inspiration's fled, i'm listless, not sure what to do next, & i just don't have it in me.

so i spend the rest of the night watering the garden & feeding my bonsai collection, cleaning up my shaping bay & even stripping & re-padding my shaping stands. anything but shaping. i've had this feeling, this 'block', before when i've made surfboards. i've had it, too, with other projects: writing, editing, painting.

i just thought i'd be honest & share this because a series of photos & neat descriptions in a swaylocks thread can sometimes be misleading. it gives the impression that building a surfboard is simply a matter of following a series of simple steps. for some people i'm sure it is (people who make boards for a living can't afford 'shaper's block') but this isn't my experience of surfboard making. for me it's more like sculpture than science, more journey than process. it's a little romantic & mysterious & somewhat bewildering. my heart, as well as my hands, has to be in it.

now i'm wondering what will happen tonight, when i go back downstairs. i'll have to wait & see.

Hey Oldy

Cant say I've had "shapers block", but I do get a bit nervous sometimes befofe I start.
I can't really afford to be wasting blanks.
What I do is think about the board I'm shaping with the measurments I want, or what someone has ordered.
I write down all my steps with measurments attached on some paper, and then stick it on the wall of my shaping bay.
This gives me a guide through steps and a reference guide if I get stuck.

Eg:

For templating:

length: 7'0"
width: 20 1/2
nose: 12 1/2
tail: 14 1/2

Planing:
Rails: As described earlier
bottom: single/double
deck: 2 1/2
rail foil: 3/4 nose 1 1/2 mid 3/4 tail

etc etc...................

This is my method. Give it a go.

Hey Oldy,

My block comes at the fin placement stage. I have no idea how to adjust for different boards and all everyone ever says is: it takes experience.

Here is my advice for shapers block:-

(1.) Its a big task to sculpt a board, with all the visualization required and the concentration to do it right. Honestly, last thing at night would be the worst time for me - too tired to get the energy to do the thing properly;

(2.) Just like me, you may be perfectionistic. This is the anchor that prevents me from getting on with the job sometimes. However, just say to yourself: "just make a start and not worry about absolute perfection". If youre anything like me, the boards turn out ok anyway, but there can always be improvement.

(3.) Maybe as stated in this thread already, you need to break the task into discrete chucks and just try to nail one or two each night:
cut and true outline,
thickness the board and adjust foil and bottom contours,
rail band bottom,
rail band top,
blend deck crown and bevels,
finness the nose and tail,
screen the top
screen the bottom
screen the rails
mark the fin position, HELP!!!!!
sign your name (about as exciting as it gets!)

One last thing: I concur with MDS, the planer is your best bet for accuracy, so use it as much as possible. For me the surform is a dirty word. It rips the foam wrecking the tint job to come. Make yourself some nice 40 grit sanding blocks instead of surform. Using the planer for top and bottom rail banding and cleaning the outline is the way to go.
Jim Phillips once said to put your plane on a next to nothing cut, that way minimising mistakes. It takes just a little longer but the result is very controllable.
I have also got the OZITO 650W and the baby one for rail banding. Its great cos its so light, you can control it with one hand.
