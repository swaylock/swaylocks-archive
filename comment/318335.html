i'm not a professional or even an amateur

but outside of technique here's the basics as I understand them.

1. the glass layer is where all the strength outside of your stringer and thickness is in the object

2. the tighter you can get this layer onto the core the stronger the end product. this is why the finish or smoothness of your core is essential for a tight glass job as any extra bump or curves along the surface are just areas for the glass layer to release. And this is because you are primarily using gravity to hold things together.

3. the optimum ratio of cloth to liquid is 1-1 so you want to remove as much of the resin from the cloth without leaving it dry but also keeping as tight against the core as you can. unfortunately you have three things going against you, the complex curves of the core, gravity, and the fact that you're trying to saturate a cloth which ia actually spun out of very thin glass fibers not something natural like bark or organic fibers with a hollow core. So technique is important.

4. you're dealing with chemical reactions so you have a limited set of time unless the resin is catalyzed by light and not chemicals. The chemical reactions are hence also affected by chemical contamination, temperature and humidity. So where, when and what you're using to glass as well as what's on the surface you are glassing plays a role.

5. as a beginner you have alot of the odds against you so to help combat those odds you'll need to be a little wasteful at the start with the following

- don't scrimpt on cloth width  before trimming try and keep the cloth weights the same

- use good scissors 12" or longer shears

- make 50% more resin than you think you'll need

- make your mixes on the slow side so you have enough time to work to pull things tight and scrape off the excess

-always work your way from the center. the center of the board out to the rail and the center of the rail out to the nose and tail

- have good lighting so you can see your work

-try to work as fast as you can but at the same time try and keep your movements in long continuous strokes

-use good brushes for the final coats

-lay your hotcoats on thick and hand sand till your surface is baby butt smooth before finishing

-use a good organic mask and multilayered gloves

- try not to move the board around till the resin sets and always go from warm to cool never from cool to warm(sun) when your letting your resin(non-uv) kick or you'll get air bubbles.

-if you can glass with UV as it smplified the process even more..

 

so if you get a decent not too long and smooth wrap cut on your cloth, use more than enough resin and pull things nice and tight and flat against the surface without exposing too much weave or getting air bubbles you should have a good start for a good end product.

color will complicate things, potentially fade and in the end make ding repair more difficult. I wouldn't use color in the beginning..

kind of it in nutshell for me.. hope it makes sense

Most of what they said. 

I "learned" how to glass from a glossy '70's pamphlet on how to make a surfboard.  It worked out ok - the nearest shapers/glassers were over a hundred miles away, and it was years before I got to hang out in a glassing room.

My only addition would be to look at the process as making a sculpture - you can add some, you take some away.

On the first lam you do, you can get to bottom/deck to look really good, you can get most of the rail area to look pretty good, then you'll have the tips and edges of the lam to deal with.  You may study up, and go w/a cut lap, or try to keep it nice and clean, but you'll probably find lots of hard resined glass in ways/shapes/places you don't want it... Don't freak, just sand it down, and do the other side.

I've taken to doing the deck first - as I find it easier to get a nice flat/smooth bottom that way (much less sanding), and I don't bother to sand the deck side lap perfectly... KISS.

I also like to wrap all the layers for structural strength and impact resistance.

I also give thanks to Greg L. every time I open a jug of RR epoxy - the extra time over regular poly is great for me and my slow hand ways, as are the curing properties v. poly "kick."

No matter what, the best advice is, just get out there and try it.  As was others experience, most of my early glass experience came from hundreds of ding repairs small to 1/2 board re-glasses.
