Hey Sean,

It looks like you are not pulling your lap "tight" enough.  Which means you have too much resin for the amount of cloth.  Try squeegeeing it a little tighter and dont be afraid to sand your lap.  I have seen two types of laminators.  One who piles resin on instead of grinding laps properly and ones who sand the board perfect before their H/C.  Hope that helps.

It may be the way you taped the lap, which is too narrow.  Tape to your cutlap line with 1" tape, then tape again using about 1/4" overlap over the inside edge of the first tape.  This will give you enough flat area to squeegee resin well past the cutlap line, removing the excess like Austin said.  When gelled enough to cut, lift up the tape along the lap line so that you can bend the cloth almost 90 degrees to the board.  Hold the blade horizontally and cut along the bend of the cloth.  Grind, file, or sand the lapline and roller it into the foam if doing the first side. This technique uses about two blades per side, and you need to time the resin to bend the tape line without the cloth coming up.  However, you don't cut into the board and it allows you to find the line when using very opaque colors.

After I'm done lapping, and everything's tight, I'll go around the board and any long strings that have been pulled out of the cloth, that are stuck to the flats, will be pulled down to let hang. I'll carefully lift the string right to where it goes into the cloth again, but just let it hang. When it's cured and ready to flip, those strings will snap right off. No worries about sanding them flush. You only have to deal with the actual lap line. If you cut your lap on a curvy part of the rail, just before it goes flat, you'll have the lap line on an apex that you can sand down easily with a block and some 80 grit without hitting the foam. If your lap's too wide, and all the way out on the flats, it's much more difficult to do.
