I'd guess the soft stall, easy going, intuitive characteristics of tubercles translate into other fin shapes as well, but I have not tried any. 

The first high aspect ratio fin I tried was the wavegrinder wg2.  When that winglet aligned with the wave energy with how the tail rocker held it,  the accelleration and speed was unmatched, but it dictated the line taken.  MrMik had made the harftub line of fins, and the first one, with the same aspect ratio as the WG2 was not as quick, but it turned on a dime, anywhere, still way quicker than a traditional dol-fin though.  This design  was quickened by fewer larger turbucles, trading a small portion of the intuitive forgiving nature for it.

Then I tried the G-whale fin, and it was as quick as the WG2, if not quicker, is extremely forgiving and intuitive.  Reriding my regular dolfin after getting used to it, was such a disappointment, waves I expected to easily make, reeled off without me.  Much harder to turn, and I had to relearn the longer slower turning radius slowing down through the whole turn.  Angle too hard parallel to shore and the fin stalled and slid towards shore abruptly and all parallel to shore momentum stalled replaced by straight to shore.  That same angle with teh Gwhale and it might drift, but the tail never lets go, or turns draggy.  Its very weird, expecting an abrupt release or instant traction, yet it just drifts and holds its line and speed.

I've cut down the top half of the Gwhale fin to about 4.5 inches depth for use on multifin shorter boards.  It works well as a thruster fin, rail fins, not so much, but it is a 50/50 foil, and should likely be deeper.  As a thruster fin is is loose, and drivey and fast, but there is the feeling of drift when pushed to its limits. but what is weird is during that transition to stall, there is plenty of warning and it never breaks loose abruptly.  When it is in the process of stalling, it is not feeling like a sea anchor  like when a regular fin stalls, and one can feel through their foot, and sometimes even hear the separation of flow.

Since I have no intention of hand shaping rail fins with tubercles I've just been making rail fins, deeper, higher aspect ratio, narrower tips,  and moved further back on the board.  They are quick and responsive, but perhaps twitchy and unstable with little to no self centering when combined with teh 0.5 gw thruster fin.  Still playing with this formula.

Riding super high aspect ratio fins makes it obvious just how much resistance to turning, and drag, the raked tip adds.

These two features are not always a bad thing.  If one were to translate it to a car at the track, the high aspect ratio fins are like 25% more power on tap, 30% less brake, with non tire squealing 4 wheel drifts through tight  turns a given.  One is not instantly going to figure it out, and may not like the feel at all.  Different is not always better, and the tubercle fins are no doubt different, and better is subjective/opinion.

I think the rubber kneed tricksters seeking airtime and forgiving landings could benefit greatly from tubercled fins.  I think the high aspect  ratio is key to speed, and this requires the fins be moved back on teh board, and likely means the fin plugs should also be moved back on the board. 

HAR fins in standard multifin box locations will likely not work with FCS2, with futures the fin will need to hang off the end of the board, and require that angle/relief to allow for it to be slid into the back of the box, reducing base area and perhaps increased interference drag.

Tubercle fins also can catch the leash, and not release it.  When the board is getting tumbled underwater and the leash catches the fin, extreme forces can happen to break fin/plug, which is of course much less likely to occur on standard fin designs.

Then there is the issue of kelp/weed, in some locations where the tubercles act like a very effective sea rake.  When i have caught a bunch of weed/seagrass near the tip of the Gwhale fin, it reminded me of my dolfin in turns and down the line speed.  Kelp often slows the board for a bit before it breaks, but I weigh 220lbs with at least 15lbs of shortboard and 25lbs of longboard.

The more "upright" the fin template is, the more "immediate" the response will be when initiating a turn, and the tighter the turning radius through the turn. With a properly tapered tip there will also be less drag than a "normal" template fin.
