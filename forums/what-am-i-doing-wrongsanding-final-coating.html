JD, if the board looked perfectly flat after applying the hotcoat and bumps appear now, I can see only one explanation: you created those bumps with the sander. It's very easy to do if you don't keep the sander moving all the time and the sanding pad as flat as possible. What I would do: apply another hot-coat, sand it flat to 120 grit. Then apply a gloss-coat and sand this up to 600 or 800 grit, starting at 320 or 400 depending on the quality of the gloss. Polishing compound and finish, you're done.

Some sanding advice for you based on my experience. Rough sand with a soft pad. Medium pads do not contour to the shape of the board. Lift your handle side up and keep it that way regardless of whether you are sanding up or down the board. You may notice that by following this method that moving in one direction cuts and the other smoothes. At least that how it works for me. The edge of the pad is the softest and that is the part that contours the most. Make as much of the perimeter of the pad touch the board as possible when sanding. This will give you a better "fit".

I use a medium pad with fresh 80 grit on the flats of both sides staying off of the edges, rails and concaves. Switch to soft pad then go over it again this time with less pressure and reaching out to the rails and the tail area concaves. I even slightly wrap the rails to just past the crown or pinline area. Then switch to 120 and do it again. Same exact sanding pattern. Hand sand rails with the worn down 80, then the worn down 120, then fresh 220. Then a fresh piece of 220 on the soft pad again using the exact same sanding pattern for the flats. From there you can make it as smooth as you want with higher grits. If I want it super smooth I'll wetsand the rails with 400 wet or dry, then using a rubber block go nose to to tail on the flats with 400 wet or dry, then same pattern and same block with purple scotchbrite and water. Comes out like satin finish. I've also never used any grit higher than 600 when polishing and that's just for dark colored boards. On clears I'll finish with 500 but this after dialing in my sanding over the course of 18 years. If you lay your gloss real flat and nice you can start with 400 when sanding for polish. Then go 500 if you can get it. 600 from there will make less work with the polisher. Lighten up your pressure progressively after the 400. 600 clogs up quick so on anything over say 6'10 I'll end up using 2 pieces 400 per side, 2 pieces 500 per side, and 2 pieces 600 per side. If it's under 6'10 it'll be 1 piece of each per side. Good luck! Jim
