Not sure what you mean about how wide your rail apex should be but for tucking the edges I copy Shaping 101 and do one band at 30 degrees, and a second band at 60 degrees. Both bands start a little bit in front of the leading fins and this is what I use to set up my bottom rail. As for the top rail, 10, 20 and 40 degrees seem to be good rounders for different rail shapes. It just depended on how far in I took each band as to what shape of rail I got.

the 'width' of your bands is going to determine the transition from the crown of your deck to the rail. the 'depth' the thickness of the rail. By apex I assume you mean what most people would refer to as radius - the thickness and shape of the rail. If so then yes it will.

If you search on here there are threads on working out tangents from a chosen curve to calculate your rail bands. As a guide 2 and 4's a good starting point for 'width', go a bit thicker than JC on his video for 'depth' on your fish and have a favourite/ similar board on hand to fondle and compare to as you round it off.

 

Good luck and enjoy

I find that the most critical measurement for rail apex diameter is the measurement from the bottom up the side of the blank. If I want a fuller rail, I'll make the mark higher....thinner, lower. No matter where you put that mark, you can still use a 2" and 4" band and get a typical "modern rail" profile. Others may disagree, but the width of the bands determines the shape of the deck more than the rail. If I'm using a Fred Tool, I'll do the bottom radius first with the Fred, then make my mark up the side. That way I can better visualize what my final rail will look like. I'll keep using the Fred until I start to "see" the profile I want. Then I'll make my mark, flip it, and measure my bands. You can play around with your angles and widths by making sketches. Break out the ruler and protractor, draw a bunch of lines, and see what you like.

Everybody has their own way. You'll develop yours. I suggest you start playing with your deck bands (the 2 and 4 inch bands) when you start doing down rails and more domed or flatter decks. But for a typical normal modern rail, 2 and 4 is a good start.

No offense intended, and to keep my kook train rollin' --- I don't even think about rail bands, save to say: I think rail bands have as much to do with production as they do with riding function. I think about 50/50, 60/40, etc. rails. Once I have the foil as I want it, I mark the where the mid point should be, and curve from there. In the nose and tail - now working with 6oz glass, and a couple layers to boot - i make sure to keep a minimum of a 1/2" 50/50, and in the tail I add a sharp putty edge later, so I can wrap the glass with some ease... Then again - on my latest board I've put in a serious step/skate deck... 1 1/2" thick rails, coming in near flat on the deck side for about 3" then stepping up to @ 4" thick in the belly, so, as you can tell, I'm not working from a crowned deck paradigm at all - my past board were flat all the way out to the rail anyway, so... Take it for what it's worth... There are many ways to sink the rail.
